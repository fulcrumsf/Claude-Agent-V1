# Skill: Storytelling & Scriptwriting

Use this skill when writing scripts, planning video structure, or crafting narration for any channel. These principles work across short-form (Shorts/Reels/TikTok) and long-form (YouTube) formats.

---

## Core Philosophy

Every video must do three things:
1. **Hook** — give the viewer a reason to keep watching (first 1–3 seconds)
2. **Build** — sustain tension and curiosity through the body
3. **Reward** — deliver a payoff that makes the viewer feel it was worth watching

If any of these three fail, viewers leave.

---

## Framework 1: Hook–Body–Reward

The universal short-form structure.

### HOOK (First 1–3 Seconds)
Open with audio, visuals, or text that immediately signals value or creates a question.
- Audio: unexpected sound, bold statement, unfinished sentence
- Visual: the most striking or bizarre frame
- Text overlay: tease the payoff without revealing it ("Wait for it..." / "This changes everything")

**Rule**: Never open with setup. Open with tension.

### BODY (Middle)
- Clear clips, fast pace, frequent cuts
- Zooms and motion keep energy high
- Every few seconds, add a **Pattern Interrupt** (see Framework 3) to reset attention
- Stack new curiosity loops before the previous one closes

### REWARD (End)
- The payoff must feel earned — it should answer what the hook promised
- End immediately after the reward. Do not linger.
- The viewer should feel something: surprise, satisfaction, laughter, awe

---

## Framework 2: Curiosity Loop

The mechanism that keeps viewers watching through the body.

**Structure**: Tease the Desire → Hold Back the Answer → Reward Curiosity

### How to Build a Loop
1. **Open the loop** — pose a question, show an incomplete scene, or make a bold claim
2. **Sustain tension** — hint at the answer without giving it away; raise the stakes
3. **Close the loop** — deliver the answer/payoff
4. **Stack loops** — open a new loop before the old one closes to chain attention forward

### Loop Tactics
- Ask a question and cut away before answering
- Show a cause but delay the effect
- Tease a transformation (before) and withhold the (after)
- Number format: "3 reasons why..." — forces viewer to stay for all 3

**Short-form rule**: One primary loop is enough. Close it at the end = the reward.

**Long-form rule**: Stack 3–5 loops throughout. Each resolved loop opens the next.

## Framework 3: Pattern Interrupt

Resets viewer attention when it starts to drift. The brain responds to change, not continuity.

### When to Use
Every 3–7 seconds in short-form. Every 30–90 seconds in long-form.

### Types of Pattern Interrupts

| Type | Example |
|------|---------|
| **Visual** | Cut angle, zoom burst, color shift, motion change |
| **Audio** | Sound effect, music drop, silence, volume spike |
| **Narrative** | Unexpected twist, contradiction, reversal |
| **Verbal** | Sudden shift in tone, unexpected word choice |
| **Text overlay** | On-screen label, stat, or reaction text |

### The Opposite Method
When the audience expects A, deliver B. The more predictable the setup, the bigger the interrupt lands. Use this as the foundation for comedy and surprise payoffs.

## Framework 4: South Park "But/Therefore" Rule

The single most important rule for causation-driven scripts.

### The Rule
Every scene or beat must connect to the next with **BUT** or **THEREFORE** — never with **AND THEN**.

| Connection | Meaning |
|------------|---------|
| **BUT** | Conflict, obstacle, reversal — something goes wrong |
| **THEREFORE** | Consequence, cause-and-effect — action leads to result |
| **AND THEN** (BANNED) | No causation — just sequential events with no tension |

### Example — Wrong (And Then)
> A cat walks into the kitchen. **And then** it knocks over the cup. **And then** the owner gets mad.

### Example — Right (But/Therefore)
> A cat walks into the kitchen. **BUT** it spots a glass on the edge. **THEREFORE** it locks eyes with the owner and slowly pushes it off. **BUT** the owner lunges forward. **THEREFORE** the cat pushes a second one.

Apply this to every transition between beats. If you can't identify a BUT or THEREFORE, the scene has no tension.

### Tension Gap
Tension is the gap between what IS and what COULD BE.
- **Open the gap**: show the character's desire vs. their current reality
- **Widen the gap**: add obstacles, raise stakes, delay resolution
- **Close the gap**: deliver the payoff

The bigger the gap before closing, the more satisfying the reward.

## Framework 5: Story Arc Structures

Use for long-form scripts, narrative compilations, or any video with a beginning/middle/end.

### 5-Stage Arc (Freytag's Pyramid)
1. **Exposition** — introduce character, setting, and normal world
2. **Rising Action** — inciting incident + escalating obstacles
3. **Climax** — the turning point; highest tension
4. **Falling Action** — aftermath; tension decreases
5. **Resolution** — new normal; loose ends tied

### 3-Act Structure (Simplified)
- **Act I (Setup)**: Who is this, what do they want, what's normal?
- **Act II (Confrontation)**: What goes wrong? What obstacles appear?
- **Act III (Resolution)**: How does it end? What changed?

### Dan Harmon's Story Circle (8 Steps)
Best for character-driven narratives:
1. A character is in a zone of comfort
2. They want something
3. They enter an unfamiliar situation
4. They adapt to it
5. They get what they wanted
6. They pay a heavy price for it
7. They return to their familiar situation
8. Having changed

### Fichtean Curve (Crisis-First)
Skips exposition — opens directly in crisis. Best for short-form or fast-paced content.
- Open with action or problem (no setup)
- Stack mini-crises throughout
- Major climax near the end
- Brief resolution

### Hero's Journey (Transformation Arc)
For longer character stories:
> Ordinary World → Call to Adventure → Refusal → Mentor → Crossing the Threshold → Tests → Ordeal → Reward → Road Back → Resurrection → Return with Elixir

Use beats selectively — not every story needs all 12 steps.

## Framework 6: Character Arc

For any video featuring a character (human, animal, mascot) with an emotional journey.

### Arc Types

| Type | Description | Use When |
|------|-------------|----------|
| **Positive Arc** | Character starts flawed, learns and grows | Transformation, underdog, triumph stories |
| **Negative Arc** | Character starts well, succumbs to flaws | Tragedy, cautionary tale, dark comedy |
| **Flat Arc** | Character doesn't change, but changes the world | Hero/role-model content, mascot-driven videos |

### 4 Stages of a Character Arc
1. **Status Quo** — character in their normal life; establish flaw, desire, or false belief
2. **Inciting Incident** — something forces them into action; their old approach is challenged
3. **Conflict & Struggle** — obstacles force confrontation with the flaw; old approach fails
4. **Aha Moment & Change** — character learns truth, shifts behavior (or refuses to and falls)

### Common Pitfalls
- **Too perfect at the start** — no flaw = no arc = no engagement
- **Unmotivated change** — arc must follow logically from obstacles encountered
- **Instant transformation** — change must feel earned, not sudden

## Framework 7: Maslow's Hierarchy for Character Motivation

Use to define what a character wants and why — creates automatic audience relatability.

### The 5 Levels (Bottom to Top)
1. **Physiological** — survival: food, water, shelter. Stakes: life or death.
2. **Safety** — security: protection from danger, financial stability, safe home.
3. **Love & Belonging** — connection: relationships, acceptance, fear of loneliness.
4. **Esteem** — respect: recognition, status, self-worth. Underdog stories live here.
5. **Self-Actualization** — potential: fulfilling purpose, creative expression, becoming who you're meant to be.

### How to Apply
- **Start low**: Begin the character at a lower level (survival, safety) for maximum stakes
- **Move up**: Each act's goal should climb toward higher needs
- **Subvert the order**: Have a character chase esteem while ignoring safety — creates tension and unexpected drama

### Storytelling Technique
> Define what your character needs at the start of the story AND why they cannot get it.

The gap between need and attainability IS the story.

## SETUP → ACTION → PAYOFF (Universal Principle)

The distilled version for short-form video prompts, scenes, and beats:

- **SETUP**: Establish the world/context quickly. Just enough to make the action meaningful.
- **ACTION**: Something unexpected, unhinged, or dramatically different from the setup.
- **PAYOFF**: The reaction, consequence, or resolution. This is what the viewer came for.

### Example
> SETUP: Cat calmly sitting on frozen lake.
> ACTION: Paw gets stuck to ice. Cat looks at camera, betrayed.
> PAYOFF: Cat drags entire frozen pond inside.

If the payoff is missing, the scene is incomplete. If the setup is too long, the viewer leaves before the action. If the action is too predictable, the payoff doesn't land.

## Short-Form Script Template

```
[HOOK — 1–3 sec]
Visual/audio/text that immediately creates a question or tension.

[LOOP OPEN — 3–10 sec]
Tease the payoff. "You won't believe what happens next." / Show the before.

[BODY — variable]
SETUP: establish context
BUT: something goes wrong / unexpected
THEREFORE: consequence escalates
BUT: it gets worse / stranger
THEREFORE: peak moment (climax)

[REWARD / PAYOFF — final 2–5 sec]
Close the loop. Deliver the promised payoff.
End immediately.
```

---

## Long-Form Script Template

```
[HOOK — 0–15 sec]
Open with the most compelling moment, question, or promise.
Do NOT start at the beginning. Start at the best part.

[LOOP STACK — 15–60 sec]
Open 2–3 curiosity loops without resolving any.
Establish character and stakes (Exposition fast).

[ACT I CLOSE — by ~15% mark]
Inciting incident. The normal world is disrupted.

[ACT II — 15%–75% mark]
Rising action. BUT/THEREFORE chain.
Pattern Interrupts every 30–90 seconds.
Close loops, open new ones. Widen the tension gap.
Character faces escalating obstacles.

[CLIMAX — ~75% mark]
Highest tension. The decision point. The turning point.

[RESOLUTION — 75%–95%]
Falling action. Payoff delivered. Character changed.

[OUTRO / CTA — final 5%]
End screen. Clear call to action if needed.
End while energy is still high.
```

## Quick Reference: Decision Matrix

| Format | Primary Framework | Arc Type | Loop Count |
|--------|------------------|----------|------------|
| TikTok/Reel/Short (< 60s) | Hook–Body–Reward + SETUP→ACTION→PAYOFF | Flat or single beat | 1 loop |
| YouTube Short (< 3 min) | Fichtean Curve + But/Therefore | Positive or flat | 1–2 loops |
| YouTube Long-form (> 5 min) | 3-Act or Dan Harmon + Tension Gap | Full character arc | 3–5 loops |
| Narrative compilation | 5-Stage Arc + SETUP→ACTION→PAYOFF per scene | Per scene: flat | 1 loop per scene |
| Educational/tutorial | Hook + numbered loops + reward | Flat (host) | 1 per section |

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Opening with setup** — start with tension, not context
- **Delayed inciting incident** — too much exposition kills momentum
- **And-Then transitions** — every beat needs BUT or THEREFORE
- **Closed loops with no new loop** — viewer has no reason to keep watching
- **Weak payoff** — if the reward doesn't match the promise of the hook, the video fails
- **Lingering after the payoff** — end immediately after the reward
- **Unmotivated character behavior** — anchor every action in a need (Maslow level)
- **No tension gap** — without a gap between desire and reality, there is no story
