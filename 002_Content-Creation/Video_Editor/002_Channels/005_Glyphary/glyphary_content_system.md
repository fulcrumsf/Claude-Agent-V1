# GLYPHARY — CHANNEL CONTENT SYSTEM

## CORE IDENTITY

- **Brand:** Glyphary
- **Tagline:** Some stories are too strange to stay buried.
- **Tone:** Quiet, eerie, slightly poetic — intellectual curiosity with a cold draft running through it
- **Audience:** Mystery/folklore enthusiasts, paranormal-curious, weird history fans, ages 16–40
- **Concept:** Folklore, mythology, collective memory, lost cities, strange coincidences, eerie historical events — told with a highly stylized visual aesthetic that matches the channel banner. Some stories are true. Some are invented. Some are the kind of thing people repeat for centuries without knowing why. X-Files energy meets literary essay. Occasionally ventures into sci-fi narratives.

## VISUAL IDENTITY

### Animation / Art Style — Multi-Style by Video
**Glyphary does not have one fixed visual style — each video gets the style that fits its story.** The channel's visual identity is the *variety* itself, unified by tone and subject matter, not by a single aesthetic. This is the defining characteristic: every thumbnail looks different, but every video feels like it belongs on the same channel.

Style options deployed per video based on story type:

- **Painterly photorealistic AI** — for WWII-era, historical, gritty political stories (example: the Berlin/Stalin/Pompeii thumbnails from the reference)
- **Cartoon / anime** — for stories with a protagonist you follow, emotional beats, or younger-skewing subject matter (example: Tunnel Rat, Iran's Revolution, The Blitz thumbnails)
- **Graphic novel / illustrated** — for crime, psychological, or literary subjects (example: The Korean Prisoner, Franz Kafka thumbnails)
- **Sci-fi infographic / card style** — for data-driven or classification-style content (example: Sleep Paralysis Classification Card)
- **3D cinematic render** — for epic or large-scale historical events

### What Unifies the Styles
- Strong typography integrated into every thumbnail — title card is part of the art
- Dark or high-contrast palettes — no pastel, no flat minimalism
- Each thumbnail reads like a **poster or book cover** for its specific story
- The subject's emotion or dramatic peak is the visual centerpiece

### Color Palette (per banner / identity)
- **Deep Plum/Ink:** `#1A0A2E` (primary brand background)
- **Amber/Rust:** `#C8923A` (aged parchment, candlelight, old maps)
- **Faded Teal:** `#4A8B8C` (cold water, old ink, the color of something forgotten)
- **Bone/Cream:** `#E8DFD0` (text, ghostly highlights)
- **Muted Sienna:** `#7A3B1E` (earth, old wood, blood that's dried)

*Individual videos may deviate from this palette — the per-video art style takes precedence.*

## CONTENT PILLARS

1. **Folklore and mythology** — origin stories, folk beliefs, the myths people actually lived by (not sanitized retellings)
2. **Collective memory and lost history** — stories passed through oral tradition that archaeology later confirmed, or that archaeology has never found
3. **Strange documented coincidences** — events so improbable they changed how people thought about fate and chance
4. **Lost cities and forgotten places** — Atlantis as one of many — real disappearances, real archaeological mysteries
5. **Invented narratives** — sci-fi, X-Files-style stories presented in the same tone — some true, some not, viewer knows the mix exists

### Tone Note
Glyphary doesn't resolve its mysteries. The wonder is in the not-knowing. Avoid conspiracy framing — this is not about who is hiding what. It's about what the universe has quietly placed in front of us that we didn't notice until now.

### The Fiction/Reality Mix
Some videos are documented history. Some are entirely invented. The channel's brand is that the line between them is the point — folklore itself has always blurred fact and myth. Signal which category a video falls into via a subtle end-card note, but let the mystery breathe during the video itself.

## THUMBNAIL SYSTEM

### Style
- **Each thumbnail is a poster or book cover for that specific story** — the art style matches the content
- Strong integrated typography — the title is part of the design, not an afterthought
- Dark, high-contrast palettes dominant across all styles
- Dramatic emotional or visual peak of the story as the central image
- Feels like a movie poster, graphic novel cover, or classified document — depending on the story

### Style Selection by Video
- Historical/political/war stories → painterly photorealistic AI (dark, cinematic, detailed faces)
- Folklore/character-led stories → cartoon or anime illustration (expressive, bold linework)
- Crime/psychological/biographical → graphic novel (stark, noir, typographic)
- Data/phenomena/classification → sci-fi infographic card layout

### Rules
**DO:**
- Match the art style to the story — don't apply one look to everything
- Short, bold title text integrated into the art — under 5 words
- The image should work as a standalone piece — you could print it

**DON'T:**
- No bright neon or clean minimal design
- No horror/gore — eerie and unsettling, not shocking
- Don't use a style that doesn't fit the story just for consistency

## TITLE SYSTEM

### Format
- Short, declarative, subtly off: "[Thing] That [Impossible Fact About It]"
- Or: "The [Lost/Forgotten/Buried] [Subject]"
- Or: "Three [Separate Events] That [Shared Something They Shouldn't]"
- Minimal emoji — occasionally a ✦ or ◈ type symbol if the title needs a separator

### Examples
- "The Map That Shows a Coast Nobody Had Visited Yet"
- "A Town That Collectively Forgot the Same Three Months"
- "Twelve Separate Cultures Remembered the Same Flood"
- "The Writing They Keep Finding Everywhere — Nobody Can Read It"
- "An Extinct Language That Appears in a Dream Journal from 1891"

### Title Patterns That Work
- `[The Thing] That [Impossible Fact]`
- `[Number] [Separate Unrelated Things] That [Shared Impossible Trait]`
- `The [Lost/Forgotten] [Subject] — [Cryptic Follow-Through]`
- `Nobody Can Explain [Specific Event/Object/Coincidence]`

---

## HOOK SYSTEM

### First 3 Seconds
- The most visually strange image in the video — static, no explanation
- Or: a single quiet sentence that opens a door: "In 1908, a cartographer submitted a map. The coastline he drew wouldn't be confirmed for another 400 years."

### First 10 Seconds
- State the mystery plainly — no dramatic music swell, no "you won't believe this"
- The strangeness of the material is the hook; trust it
- End the opening with the unanswered question — and don't answer it yet
